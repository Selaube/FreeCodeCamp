John von Neumann

a genius who was comfortable integrating both pure and applied sciences

John von Neumann, the man on the right, made major contributions to a number of fields.

Here's a time line of John von Neumann's life:

  • 1903 - Born in Budapest, Kingdom of Hungary
  • 1925 - Received his degree in Chemical Engineering at the Swiss Federal Institute in Zurich
  • 1925 - His work “An Axiomatization of Set Theory” demonstrated axiomatic set theory and logics, problems in quantum mechanics, and special mathematical functions
  • 1926 - Also went on to obtain his Doctorate in mathematics at University of Budapest
  • 1928 - Published “Theory of Parlor Games,” a key paper in the field of game theory. "Game Theory" focused on elements of bluffing and probability theory
  • 1932 - Gave a lecture at IAS in 1932: “On Certain Equations of Economics and a Generalization of Brouwer’s Fixed Point Theorem." It was about seminal contribution to linear and nonlinear programming in economics and this was later published in 1937
  • 1933 - Became one of the first professors at the Institute for Advanced Study (IAS), Princeton, New Jersey
  • 1940 - Introduced a theory of rings of operators in his development of mathematical techniques suited to quantum phenomena
  • 1943 - J. Robert Oppenheimer invited John to work on the Manhattan Project. He helped work on the hydrodynamics and implosion design for an atomic bomb
  • 1944 - Modified the U.S. Army’s hard-wired ENIAC to run as a stored-program machine. Later, he then lobbied to build an improved computer at IAS.
  • 1946 - Along with Klaus Fuchs, John filed a secret patent on "Improvement in Methods and Means for Utilizing Nuclear Energy." It outlined the specifications of using a fission bomb to compress fusion fuel for nuclear implosion
  • 1950 - Became a consultant to the Weapons Systems Evaluation Group (WSEG). He advised the Joint Chiefs of Staff and the US Secretary of Defense for developing new technologies and nuclear weapons
  • 1954 - For two years, Served as a member of the Atomic Energy Commission. He was an architect of the policy of nuclear deterrence. This was developed by President Dwight D. Eisenhower’s administration
  • 1956 - Received the Medal for Freedom in 1956. Also, he received the Enrico Fermi Award and Albert Einstein Commemorative Award
  • 1957 - died, 18 months after diagnosed with cancer

"He was a pioneer of the application of operator theory to quantum mechanics in the development of functional analysis, and a key figure in the development of game theory and the concepts of cellular automata, the universal constructor and the digital computer."
